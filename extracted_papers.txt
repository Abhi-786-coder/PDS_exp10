Abstract
The increasing demand for accurate toxicity assessment and to minimise or eliminate the use of animal testing has accelerated
the development of numerous computational models, AI/ML models, and online resources that support research in computa-
tional toxicology. This review addresses toxicity prediction and chemical safety evaluation, focusing on computational models
and data coverage, Molecular Descriptors, QSAR models, AI/ML-based approaches, Explainable AI, predictive methodologies,
regulatory relevance, and accessibility. This collectively enables the identification, prediction, and analysis of chemical toxicity
across various biological endpoints. In addition, the review highlights AI/ML tools for predicting toxicity endpoints, such as
neurotoxicity, hepatotoxicity, cardiotoxicity, genotoxicity, and environmental toxicity. Regulatory limitations vary significantly
among countries and jurisdictions, exhibiting a marked absence of convergence. Current debates regarding regulatory norms
focus on achieving global conformity. Regulatory adaptability is the key as AI evolves rapidly. The promotion of AI/ML tool
integration and interoperable frameworks could substantially enhance the future of predictive toxicology.

Challenges and limitations
Toxicity prediction is often resource-intensive, time-consum-
ing, and associated with ethical considerations (Yang et al.
2018). To overcome these challenges, computational methods
are being adapted to allow researchers to assess toxicological
risks. These methods include a variety of techniques such as
quantitative structure–activity relationships (QSAR), read-
across strategies, and pharmacokinetic modelling (Ahlberg
et al. 2014). In recent years, AI/ML approaches have facili-
tated various toxicity predictions (Cavasotto and Scardino
2022). Although computational and AI/ML models have
made significant strides in toxicity prediction, several limi-
tations remain. A major concern is the quality and availability
of toxicological data, which can be incomplete, inconsistent,
or poorly documented. This not only impacts model training
but also reduces reliability. Additionally, many datasets are
imbalanced, with a large proportion of non-toxic samples,
which can potentially lead to biased predictions. A major
challenge is the lack of high-quality, consistent and suffi-
ciently large datasets, making it difficult to develop reliable
toxicity prediction models (Guo et al. 2023). Another chal-
lenge is that these models often do not perform well outside
the specific chemical datasets on which they were trained,
making it difficult to apply them to new chemicals. The lack
of standardised methods for building and validating models
also makes it difficult to compare and reduces confidence in
the predictive performance. Furthermore, many models are
purely data-driven and do not consider the underlying bio-
logical mechanisms, which can lead to inaccurate predictions
when dealing with complex biological systems. Due to these
issues, regulatory agencies remain cautious about using AI-
based models, primarily because of concerns over transpar-
ency, reproducibility, and the lack of robust validation frame-
works. To improve accuracy, better integration of diverse
data sources is needed through standardised preprocessing
and multimodal methods like ensemble methods or federated
